# Purchase Council Example

> Outputs below are representative, not deterministic. On-device models may
> produce different wording or emphasis across runs, even for the same input.

## Question

> Should I buy a used road bike for $800?

## What the council sees

- **Values:** frugality, fitness, low-maintenance living.
- **Goals:** commute by bike at least three days a week.
- **Boundaries:** no high-interest debt; discretionary purchases under $1,000 require a pause.
- **Memory:** user already owns a 2019 hybrid bike in good condition.

## Representative perspective output

### Summary

A used road bike could improve your commute speed and enjoyment, but the $800 spend only makes sense if you will ride enough to offset the cost of keeping your current hybrid. The upgrade is a want, not a need, unless the hybrid is genuinely holding you back.

### Trade-offs

- **Comfort and speed vs. maintenance risk.** A road bike is faster, but a used drivetrain and wheels carry unknown service costs.
- **Upfront cost vs. reduced transit fares.** Daily bike commuting could repay the $800 over a year, but only if the habit sticks.
- **New motivation vs. storage and upkeep.** A new machine can rekindle the habit, or it can become an unused asset that needs tune-ups.

### Blind spots

- Frame and component wear have not been inspected in person.
- Resale value is unknown if you stop riding.
- Your current hybrid might deliver most of the benefit with a $200 wheel and tire upgrade.
- Weather and theft risk where you park have not been factored in.

### Dissent

- **Frugal:** "A $250 service on your hybrid likely gives 80% of the commuting benefit without the $800 outlay."
- **Future Self:** "If winter storage breaks the habit, this becomes an expensive decoration by spring."
- **Systems Thinker:** "Budget for helmet, lights, lock, and annual maintenance before comparing the true cost."
